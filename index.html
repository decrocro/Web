Chapter 1: Project Management Concepts

Attributes of a Project
  • A project is a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result.
  • Temporary means every project has a definite beginning and end.
  • Unique purpose means the project is not a routine operation and produces a specific deliverable.
  • Progressive elaboration means projects are developed in steps and continue by increments.
  • Projects consume resources such as people, money, hardware and software.
  • A project usually has a primary sponsor or champion who provides direction and funding.
  • Projects involve uncertainty because every project is unique and carries risks or unknowns.
Project Life Cycle
  • The project life cycle provides a framework for managing a project from initiation to closure.
  • Initiation defines the project objectives, scope and feasibility.
  • Planning develops a detailed plan for how the project will be executed.
  • Execution carries out the tasks defined in the project plan.
  • Monitoring and Controlling tracks progress, identifies deviations and takes corrective action.
  • Closure formalizes acceptance and hands over the final product.
Project Management Process
  • Starting / Initiation: define the project at a high level and obtain authorization.
  • Organizing and Preparing / Planning: develop the roadmap including scope, schedule and budget.
  • Carrying out the Work / Execution: the phase where the main project work is completed.
  • Monitoring and Controlling: track performance, identify variances, manage risks and report progress.
  • Closing: formalize acceptance and hand over the final product.
Benefits of Project Management
  • Improves planning and organization.
  • Helps control time, cost, scope, quality and risk.
  • Improves communication with stakeholders.
  • Helps detect problems early through monitoring and controlling.
  • Supports successful delivery of project objectives.

Chapter 2: The Project Team

What is a Project Team?
  • A project team is a group of individuals working on a specific project with a similar objective.
  • Each team member is responsible for assigned tasks and contributes to project success.
  • Organizations create project teams to improve processes, solve problems and achieve goals.
Team Development Stages
  • Forming: team members are uncertain and get acquainted with the project goals.
  • Storming: differences in opinions and working styles appear, causing possible conflict.
  • Norming: the team establishes norms and processes and understands each member's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Performing: the team operates at peak performance with collaboration and focus.
  • Adjourning: the team dissolves after project completion.
Effective Project Team Attributes
  • Clear goals and objectives.
  • Open communication.
  • Mutual trust and respect.
  • Collaboration and cooperation.
  • Commitment and accountability.
  • Effective leadership.
  • Constructive conflict resolution.
Common Team Problems
  • Poor communication can cause misunderstandings, errors and delays.
  • Lack of clear goals creates confusion and frustration.
  • Interpersonal conflicts happen because of different perspectives and constraints.
  • Insufficient resources can cause team dysfunction.
Ethical Behavior
  • Ethical behavior is important for trust and credibility in project management.
  • Project managers and team members should promote honesty, integrity, fairness and responsibility.
Conflict on Project
  • Conflict is inevitable in project environments.
  • Conflict can be managed constructively if handled openly and fairly.
  • Good conflict management can strengthen teamwork and decision-making.
Problem Solving and Brainstorming
  • Problem solving helps project teams identify root causes and choose effective solutions.
  • Brainstorming generates many ideas before evaluating them.
  • Rules include defer judgment, encourage wild ideas, quantity over quality and build on others' ideas.
Time Management
  • Time management involves planning, scheduling, monitoring and controlling project tasks.
  • It helps the project finish on time, within budget and according to project goals.
  • Key aspects include planning, scheduling, resource allocation, monitoring, control and task management.

Chapter 3: Project Initiation & Stakeholder

Project Initiation
  • Project initiation is the first phase of project management.
  • It defines the project's purpose, scope and feasibility.
  • It secures approval from key stakeholders.
  • Core activities include defining goals, scope and constraints, identifying stakeholders, appointing the project manager and securing funding.
  • The purpose is to confirm business need, define high-level scope, identify stakeholders and decide whether to proceed, delay or stop.
Key Outputs from Project Initiation
  • Business case: justifies the project's feasibility, benefits and costs.
  • Project charter: outlines the project's purpose, high-level scope and goals to gain approval.
  • Initial stakeholder list: documents key stakeholders, their expectations and roles.
Business Case
  • A business case explains the problem statement, objectives and benefits.
  • Problem statement explains what problem is being solved and what happens if nothing is done.
  • Objectives explain what the project aims to achieve and should be clear and measurable.
  • Benefits may include business value, cost reduction, revenue improvement, risk reduction or compliance improvement.
Stakeholders
  • A stakeholder is any individual, group or organization that can affect or be affected by the project.
  • Stakeholders may define requirements, provide feedback and validate the solution.
  • Internal stakeholders include management, project team, IT/security team and end users.
  • External stakeholders include vendors, customers, suppliers, regulators and auditors.
Stakeholder Analysis
  • Stakeholder analysis identifies and assesses stakeholder influence, interest and impact.
  • It helps create communication and engagement strategies.
  • Power/Interest Grid categories are: manage closely, keep satisfied, keep informed and monitor.
  • High power/high interest stakeholders should be managed closely.
  • High power/low interest stakeholders should be kept satisfied.
  • Low power/high interest stakeholders should be kept informed.
  • Low power/low interest stakeholders should be monitored with minimum effort.
Project Charter
  • A project charter formally authorizes a project's existence.
  • It defines the project purpose, scope, objectives and key participants.
  • It gives the project manager authority to use resources.
  • It helps align stakeholders and prevent scope creep.
  • Components include project title, purpose, objectives, success criteria, scope, deliverables, stakeholders, roles, timeline, budget, risks and assumptions.

Chapter 3: Project Planning & Controlling

SMART Objectives
  • Objectives define what needs to be achieved.
  • Specific: clear and unambiguous.
  • Measurable: progress can be quantified.
  • Achievable: realistic within resources.
  • Time-bound: has defined deadlines.
Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
  • WBS is a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work.
  • The 100% rule means the WBS includes 100% of the work defined by the project scope.
  • Root is the final project deliverable.
  • Phases are major logical groupings.
  • Work packages are the lowest-level manageable activities.
RACI Matrix
  • RACI clarifies roles and responsibilities for each task.
  • R means Responsible: the doer.
  • A means Accountable: the owner of the result.
  • C means Consulted: two-way communication.
  • I means Informed: one-way communication.
Scheduling and Duration Estimates
  • Scheduling determines the start and finish dates for project activities.
  • Steps include identifying activities, sequencing activities, estimating resources and estimating durations.
  • Expert judgment is based on historical experience.
  • Analogous estimation compares with a similar past project.
  • Parametric estimation uses statistical relationships.
  • Three-point estimation formula is (Optimistic + 4 × Most Likely + Pessimistic) ÷ 6.
Network Logic
  • A network diagram shows logical relationships and dependencies between activities.
  • Nodes represent activities.
  • Arrows show dependencies.
  • A path is a sequence of connected activities.
  • Network logic helps identify the critical path and project duration.
Forward Pass and Backward Pass
  • Forward pass determines earliest start and earliest finish times.
  • ES means Early Start.
  • EF means Early Finish.
  • EF = ES + Duration.
  • When activities merge, the successor ES is the maximum EF of all predecessors.
  • Backward pass determines latest start and latest finish times without delaying the project.
  • LF means Late Finish.
  • LS means Late Start.
  • LS = LF - Duration.
  • When tracing backward, the predecessor LF is the minimum LS of all successors.
Critical Path, Slack and Gantt Chart
  • Slack is the amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project end date.
  • Slack = LS - ES.
  • Critical path is the longest path through the network diagram.
  • Critical path determines the shortest time to complete the project.
  • Activities on the critical path have zero slack.
  • A Gantt chart is a horizontal bar chart that illustrates the project schedule.

Chapter 4: Cost Planning & Performance

Project Cost Estimates
  • Accurate cost estimates are the foundation of project success.
  • Cost estimates determine feasibility and secure funding.
  • Direct costs include salaries, software licenses and servers.
  • Indirect costs include overhead, office rent and utilities.
  • Fixed cost example: one-time hardware purchase.
  • Variable cost example: monthly cloud usage fees.
Project Budgeting
  • Project budgeting aggregates estimated costs to establish an authorized cost baseline.
  • Allocate total estimate to individual WBS work packages.
  • Aggregate work package costs to determine total budgeted cost.
  • Add contingency reserves to form the final cost baseline.
Cumulative Budgeted Cost / S-Curve
  • Project costs are not spent linearly.
  • Budgeting involves a time-phased view of costs.
  • Cumulative costs over time create an S-Curve.
  • Start: low spending during planning.
  • Middle: peak spending during execution/development.
  • End: spending tapers off during closure.
Actual Cost and Committed Cost
  • Actual Cost (AC) is the total cost actually incurred and recorded for work performed.
  • Examples of AC include salaries paid, invoices settled and software subscriptions paid.
  • Committed cost means funds obligated but not yet paid.
  • Examples include signed purchase orders and vendor contracts.
  • Committed cost helps prevent accidental overspending.
Earned Value and Cost Performance
  • Earned Value (EV) is the estimated value of work actually completed.
  • EV = % Complete × Budget at Completion (BAC).
  • Spending 50% of money does not mean the project is 50% complete.
  • Cost Variance (CV) is the budget deficit or surplus at a point in time.
  • Negative CV means over budget.
  • Cost Performance Index (CPI) measures cost efficiency.
  • CPI less than 1.0 means cost overrun.
Managing Cash Flow
  • Cost performance is about budget.
  • Cash flow is about having money in the bank to pay bills.
  • Inflow includes milestone payments from clients.
  • Outflow includes salaries and hardware purchases.
  • IT projects may have large upfront outflows before inflows arrive.

2.1 Project Team Development and Effectiveness

Project teams, like any group, evolve through distinct stages. Understanding these stages allows project managers to anticipate challenges and facilitate team growth:

Forming: 
This initial stage is characterized by uncertainty and anxiety as team members get acquainted and learn about the project goals. 
Storming: 
As team members become more comfortable, differences in opinions and working styles emerge, leading to potential conflict. 
Norming: 
The team begins to establish norms and processes for working together. Members start to understand and appreciate each other's strengths and weaknesses.
Performing: 
The team operates at its peak performance. Members are highly collaborative, autonomous, and focused on achieving project goals. 
Adjourning: 
This final stage involves the dissolution of the team after the project is completed.

Stakeholders

A stakeholder in an IT project is any individual, group, or organization that can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by the project’s decisions, activities, or outcomes. They have a vested interest in the IT project's success and range from project teams and sponsors to end-users and suppliers.

Role in IT Projects
They define project requirements, provide feedback on software features, and validate that the technology solves the intended business problems.

Identifying stakeholders early helps avoid issues like scope creep, conflicting requirements, and project failure.

Stakeholders Analysis

Stakeholder analysis is a critical project management process that identifies and assesses the influence, interests, and impact of individuals or groups involved in a project. It maps stakeholders based on their power and interest to create targeted communication and engagement strategies, ensuring alignment, minimizing risks, and securing project support.

Why need to analyze?

Slide 12: Key Steps in Stakeholder Analysis
Key Steps in Stakeholder Analysis

Identify Stakeholders: Brainstorm all individuals, teams, or organizations affected by or capable of influencing the project (e.g., customers, executives, developers).

Understand & Analyze: Determine their needs, expectations, and potential impact on the project.

Prioritize (Map): Use a Power/Interest Grid to classify stakeholders into four groups to determine the level of engagement
High Power/High Interest: Manage closely (key players).
High Power/Low Interest: Keep satisfied.
Low Power/High Interest: Keep informed.
Low Power/Low Interest: Monitor with minimum effort.

Develop Engagement Strategy :  Plan how to communicate and manage relationships based on their mapping

Slide 15: Project Charter
Project Charter

A project charter is a concise, formal document that officially authorizes a project's existence, outlining its purpose, scope, objectives, and key participants. Serving as a foundational roadmap, it defines the project manager's authority, aligns stakeholders on goals, and secures necessary resources. It acts as an elevator pitch for the project. 

Purpose & Benefits : 
Authorization – Give the PM the authority to apply resources.
Alignment – Ensures everyone involved has a shared understanding of the project’s purpose and direction.
Reference point – Serves as a guide for decision-making throughout the project lifecycle.
Efficiency – Prevents “scope creep” by clearly defining boundaries early on.
Slide 16: Project Charter
Project Charter

Key Components of Project Charter :
Project Title & Purpose : The project’s name and justification (why it is being done).
Objectives & Success Criteria : Measurable goals and how success will be measured.

Stakeholders & Roles : Key individuals involved, including the sponsor and project manager.
High-Level timeline & Budget : Estimated milestones and resource constraints.
Risk & Assumptions : Known constraints and potential risks.

Slide 3: 3.2 Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
3.2 Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

Decomposition of Work

The WBS is a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team.

The 100% Rule: The WBS includes 100% of the work defined by the project scope.

Root: The final project deliverable (e.g., "Mobile App").

Phases: Major logical groupings (Design, Dev, Test).

Work Packages: The lowest level, manageable activities.

Slide 6: Project Scheduling
Project Scheduling

Scheduling determines the start and finish dates for project activities. It is the roadmap for execution.

Estimation Techniques

Identify activities (from WBS).

Sequence activities (Dependencies).

Estimate resources & durations.

Expert Judgment: Based on historical experience.

Analogous: Comparing to a similar past project (Top-down).

Parametric: Statistical relationship (e.g., hours per line of code).

Three-Point: (Optimistic + 4*MostLikely + Pessimistic) / 6.

Slide 7: Network Logic
Network Logic

A network diagram visually displays the logical relationships (dependencies) between project activities.

This allows us to identify the critical path and calculate project duration.

Nodes: Represent activities (in PDM - Precedence Diagramming Method).

Arrows: Show dependencies (A must finish before B starts).

Path: A sequence of connected activities.

3.5 Developing the Network
Slide 8: Concepts
Concepts

The Forward Pass determines the earliest times an activity can start and finish based on network logic.

Formulas

ES (Early Start): The earliest point an activity can begin.

EF (Early Finish): ES + Duration.

Rule: When activities merge, the ES of the successor is the MAX EF of all predecessors.

3.4.3 Forward Pass Calculation
Slide 9: Concepts
Concepts

The Backward Pass determines the latest times an activity can start and finish without delaying the project.

Formulas

LF (Late Finish): The latest point an activity can end.

LS (Late Start): LF - Duration.

Rule: When tracing back, the LF of a predecessor is the MIN LS of all successors.

3.5.1 Gantt Charts

Visualizing the Schedule

A Gantt chart is a horizontal bar chart used to illustrate the project schedule.

Modern tools like Microsoft Project automate the creation of Gantt charts from WBS and dependency data.

Axis: Time is on the X-axis; Activities are on the Y-axis.

Bars: Length represents duration.

Utility: Easy for stakeholders to understand progress and overlaps.

Slide 2: Importance of Costing
Importance of Costing

Accurate cost estimates are the foundation of project success. They determine feasibility and secure funding.

Types of Costs in IT

Understanding cost behavior is crucial for accurate estimation.

Feasibility: Can we afford to do this project?

Baseline: Creates a standard to measure performance against.

Funding: Justifies the investment to stakeholders.

Direct Costs: Salaries, Software Licenses, Servers.

Indirect Costs: Overhead, Office Rent, Utilities.

Fixed vs. Variable: One-time hardware purchase vs. monthly cloud usage fees.

4.1 Project Cost Estimates
Slide 3: The process of aggregating estimated costs to establish an authorized cost baseline.
The process of aggregating estimated costs to establish an authorized cost baseline.

1. Allocate Total

Distribute the total project estimate to individual work packages in the WBS.

2. Aggregate

Sum the costs of work packages to determine the total Budgeted Cost.

3. Authorize

Add contingency reserves to form the final Cost Baseline.

4.2 Project Budgeting: Allocation
Slide 4: 4.2 Cumulative Budgeted Cost
4.2 Cumulative Budgeted Cost

The S-Curve

Project costs are not spent linearly. Budgeting involves developing a time-phased view of costs.

This accumulation of costs over time creates the characteristic "S-Curve" used for monitoring performance.

Start: Low spending (Planning).

Middle: Peak spending (Execution/Development).

End: Tapering off (Closure).

Slide 6: Actual Cost (AC)
Actual Cost (AC)

The total cost actually incurred and recorded in accomplishing work performed during a given time period.

Committed Cost

Funds that have been obligated or encumbered but not yet paid. Critical for IT procurement.

Salaries paid.

Invoices settled.

Software subscriptions paid.

Signed Purchase Orders (POs) for hardware.

Contracts signed with external vendors.

Why important? Prevents accidental overspending of the "remaining" budget.

Slide 9: Cost Variance (CV)
Cost Variance (CV)

The amount of budget deficit or surplus at a given point in time.

Result: Negative = Over Budget.

Cost Performance Index (CPI)

A measure of the cost efficiency of budgeted resources.

Result: < 1.0 = Cost Overrun.

4.5 Cost Performance Analysis
Slide 10: 4.5 Managing Cash Flow
4.5 Managing Cash Flow

Liquidity is King

Cost performance is about budget; Cash flow is about having money in the bank to pay bills.

Project Managers must align the Project Schedule with the Funding Limit Reconciliation.

Inflow: Milestones payments from client.

Outflow: Monthly salaries, hardware purchases.

The Gap: IT projects often have large upfront outflows (hardware) before inflows arrive.
